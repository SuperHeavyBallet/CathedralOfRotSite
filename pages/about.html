About The Cathedral of Rot

The Cathedral of Rot is a sprawling, interconnected universe of subterranean decay, absurd philosophy, and crumbling empires. Set deep beneath the surface of a forgotten world, it is a place where language holds power, fungus breathes life, and fractured species claw meaning from the ruins of older civilizations.

At its heart, The Cathedral of Rot explores the collision of ideology, biology, and belief within an ancient mountain hollowed out by time, ambition, and rot. Through novels, stories, bestiaries, maps, and field journals, the world is slowly uncovered—not in linear order, but in fragmented testimonies, delusional chronicles, and half-remembered truths.

The Core Narrative

The flagship work of the universe is the novel The Hollow Bleed, a darkly satirical fantasy following Markus Brehn, a Skarn observer, as he journeys downward through the mountain's many layers. What begins as academic curiosity soon dissolves into horror, rebellion, and revelation as Markus encounters the complex under-societies of the mountain: the warring Dwarvs, the passive Dwarfs, the secretive Word Keepers, and the reemerging Dwarg elite.

Through his descent, Markus discovers:

  • Bizarrre fungal ecosystems with strange and sometimes semi-sentient properties.
  • Bureaucratic absurdity and caste conflict between biologically identical but ideologically divided peoples.
  • A metaphysical force known as The First Word, capable of shaping reality through precise language.
  • Ancient ruins left by a vanished race known only as The Elders, whose collapse may have unbalanced the world itself.

Key Themes:

  • Decay and Power – Institutions rot, but cling to form. Language ossifies. Authority is often absurd but still dangerous.
  • Fungus and Thought – Fungal life is not mere background; it infects, enlightens, and connects. Air, dream, and rotfungus form the biological and spiritual spine of the world.
  • Fragmented Reality – Truth is subjective, biased by memory, word, and translation. Many texts within the Cathedral contradict one another.
  • Satirical Horror – Bureaucracies that suffocate, religious orders that lose the point, ideologies that loop back into madness—all presented with biting dark humor.

The Dwarg Lineage:

At the center of the mountain's socio-political history lies the fragmented lineage of the Dwargs:

  • Dwargs: The original subterranean species, gifted with language, split by ideology.
  • Foolish, power-hungry descendants who claim superiority through ‘education’.
  • Dwarfs: Peaceful, practical folk who simply exist within the mountain.
  • Word Keepers: Isolated, monastic Dwargs devoted to cataloging and ‘perfecting’ language itself.
  • The Returned Dwargs: A hidden technocratic faction, reemerging with dreams of absolute order through intellectual domination.

These factions, alongside the Skarn and countless mutated, fungal, and forgotten beings, create a deep tangle of allegiances, myths, and revolutions beneath the stone.

The Cathedral of Rot is not a single story—it is a decaying archive. A mountain of conflicting voices. A slow infection. A growing body of fiction that expands downward, not outward.

You are invited to descend.

My Inspirations

Ever since the mid 90's, I have been enthralled by the dark, gritty, gothic and often hillariously horrible worlds that human minds have so meticulously conjured.

Films:

    Surreal Bureaucracy & Absurd Dystopias
  • Brazil (1985, Terry Gilliam) – For its dreamlike, suffocating bureaucracy, malfunctioning machines, and the overwhelming absurdity of order.
  • The Platform (2019) – For its layered, vertical architecture and social hierarchy embodied by spatial structure.
  • Stalker (1979, Andrei Tarkovsky) – Slow, philosophical descent into a mysterious zone, where meaning is ambiguous and dangers are metaphysical.
  • THX 1138 (1971, George Lucas) – Subterranean sterility, loss of identity, and rebellion against enforced conformity.
  • Naked Lunch (1991) – A surreal, paranoid plunge into drug addiction, insect typewriters, and bureaucratic madness where language and identity melt into each other.
  • 12 Monkeys (1995) – A time-twisting dystopia where plague, prophecy, and memory collide, challenging the stability of cause and self.
  • The Matrix (1999) – A simulated reality maintained through linguistic and cognitive control, where escape begins with seeing the system.
  • Escape from New York (1981) – A grimy, decayed future where authority and criminality blur, and survival demands cynicism and calculation.
    • Organic Horror, Fungal Invasion, and Body-Ecosystems
  • Annihilation (2018, Alex Garland) – For its dreamlike decay, sentient ecology, and deeply personal transformation through contact with alien biology.
  • The Thing (1982, John Carpenter) – Paranoia, identity breakdown, and a grotesque mutative organism echo the threat of Rotfungus or Mindthieves.
  • Matango (Attack of the Mushroom People) (1963) – Campy but foundational mushroom horror; the transformation of mind and body via fungal life.
  • Color Out of Space (2019) – Cosmic contamination and the slow corruption of both environment and language.
  • Event Horizon (1997) – Cosmic horror through technological resurrection; a ship becomes a vessel for hellish, psychic infestation.
  • Johnny Got His Gun (1971) – A silent scream from a body robbed of senses; internal monologue and disconnection create an existential living death.
  • Kingdom Hospital (2004) – A surreal medical horror series where bureaucracy, ghosts, and madness operate through corridors of both flesh and stone.
    • Subterranean or Enclosed Worldbuilding
  • City of Lost Children (1995, Jeunet & Caro) – A decaying, surreal city of strange technology and grotesque characters, with childlike logic and nightmare logic.
  • The Descent (2005) – Claustrophobic caves, fear of the unknown, primal regression, and themes of madness.
  • Timecrimes (2007) – Temporal loops and paranoia; useful inspiration for Untiming or recursive causality.
  • Dark City (1998) – An underground manipulated world, false memories, and architecture as ideology.
  • A Boy and His Dog (1975) – A post-apocalyptic satire where underground societies operate on twisted logics, revealing the rot beneath civility.
  • The Road (2009) – A bleak, intimate survival story where the world’s death is mirrored in every step and word between father and son.
  • The Walking Dead (2010–) – A long, slow collapse of society where human ideology decays faster than the dead flesh walking among them.
    • Philosophical Sci-Fi & Thought-Contagion
  • Upstream Color (2013, Shane Carruth) – Mind control, cyclical trauma, and metaphysical links between humans and unseen biological forces.
  • The Fountain (2006) – Multiple overlapping timelines, fungus and decay as symbols of spiritual transcendence and loss.
  • Beyond the Black Rainbow (2010) – Hypnotic pacing, retrofuturist dread, philosophical stagnation as horror.
  • Eraserhead (1977, David Lynch) – Deeply symbolic body horror and the suffocation of industrial-existential anxiety.
  • Solaris (1972) – A meditative sci-fi drama about grief and memory, where an alien ocean resurrects personal ghosts to question reality.
  • Twin Peaks (1990–1991) – A surreal small-town mystery where dreams bleed into waking life, and language cannot contain the lurking Other.
  • Jin-Roh: The Wolf Brigade (1999) – A melancholic political thriller where ideology and identity become inseparable beneath the mask of the state.
    • Decay, Mutation, and Cultural Collapse
  • Come and See (1985) – For psychological unraveling under oppressive systems; visual descent into madness and horror without supernatural cause.
  • Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind (1984) – Toxic fungal ecosystems, decaying technology, and moral ambiguity in ancient, collapsed worlds.
  • Delicatessen (1991, Jeunet & Caro) – Black humor, decayed society, and grotesque rituals of survival.
  • The Lighthouse (2019) – Isolation, mythic madness, obsessive loops of duty and communication breakdown.
  • Aguirre: The Wrath of God (1972) – A descent into madness and obsession through jungle ruin, where the dream of dominion ends in delusion.
  • Akira (1988) – A visionary explosion of psychic mutation and civil collapse where power corrupts not just the body, but meaning itself.
  • The Wicker Man (1973) – A rural horror where pagan ritual hides cruelty beneath tradition, and belief devours reason.
    • High Fantasy & Mythic Surrealism
  • The Dark Crystal (1982) – A fantasy of decay and prophecy where divided races reflect a world losing its spiritual cohesion.
  • Labyrinth (1986) – A dream-logic fairytale where identity, control, and coming of age are twisted through symbolic landscapes.
  • Neverending Story (1984) – A metafictional fantasy where stories shape worlds, and the absence of belief becomes an apocalyptic force.
  • The Lord of the Rings (2001–2003) – A mythic epic where descent into darkness is matched by the burden of memory and legacy.
  • The Hobbit (1977/2012–2014) – A lighter but symbolically rich journey into the unknown, where comfort clashes with the call to transform.
  • Apocalypto (2006) – A visceral depiction of civilizational collapse where human ritual and brutality overshadow the jungle’s raw indifference.
  • El Dorado (Various) – A myth of unreachable utopia, where obsession with gold and glory leads to ruin and erasure.
    • Meta-Narrative, Language, and Archives
  • Synecdoche, New York (2008) – Recursive identity, art and life blending, decay through over-interpretation.
  • The Ninth Configuration (1980) – Spiritual crisis, madness, and the fluid boundary between delusion and reality.
  • Enemy (2013) – Psychological doubles, the fragility of identity, and mysterious symbolic systems.
  • The Congress (2013) – Language, identity commodification, and fragmented realities.
